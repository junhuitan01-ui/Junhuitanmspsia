Differential Research Programme (DRP) with Centre for Urban Sustainability (CUS)

The Centre for Urban Sustainability's Differential Research Programme allows selected students to participate in lecturer supervised research exploring how cement and concrete can be engineered to conduct and store electricity. By incorporating innovative materials such as recycled carbon, graphene, carbon nanotubes, and biochar, students investigate how future buildings and infrastructure could serve as large scale energy storage systems.

Build the Battery of the Future with Concrete

Cement and concrete can be made to conduct and store electricity like a battery when mixed with additives like recycled carbon, graphene, carbon nanotubes, or biochar. Although they may not store as much power as a conventional battery, the fact that there is so much cement used in urban environments makes it worth considering it as energy storage to save overall space, weight and cost. In this project, students will have the opportunity to explore making cement "batteries" and learn how batteries are tested.

Advanced Materials for the Built Environment

Dr Wong Sook Fun (second from right), and Mr Kevin Lee (second from left)

Laboratory evaluation of recycled mixed-plastic materials for construction use.

Plastic waste remains one of today's greatest environmental challenges, particularly when different plastics are mixed or contaminated and difficult to recycle. Researchers at the Centre for Urban Sustainability (CUS) are developing innovative ways to transform these materials into high-value construction products.

Supported by the National Environment Agency (NEA), CUS has developed a process that uses Near-Infrared (NIR) technology to identify and sort mixed plastics before incorporating them into asphalt pavements and concrete. This research reduces landfill waste, supports the circular economy, and improves the performance of sustainable construction materials.

Working alongside industry partner Samwoh Corporation, students and researchers are evaluating these materials through real-world road pavement and concrete footpath trials, helping shape the future of greener and more sustainable urban infrastructure.
